Team comparison

This table reveals some interesting information: following the sequence of Team1, Team2, Team3, and Team4, the transactions’ block height do not overlap (the block height of Team1’s attack does not overlap with Team2’s, Team2’s does not overlap with Team3’s, and Team3’s does not overlap with Team4’s)

Team1 carried out the attack immediately after receiving funds from the Railgun. On the other hand, Team2 obtained their funds more than ten days earlier and began their attack after Team1 finished theirs. This raises an interesting point: could Team1 and Team2 be the same team or have some connection?

Let’s assume that Team4 is also an MEV/Bot. Team3 and Team4 initiated their attacks’ a considerable time after Team1 and Team2. This happened because Team1 and Team2’s attacks did not exhaust all the profits, leaving some behind. Team3’s algorithm was clearly not as effective as Team4’s, with Team4 conducting 9 attacks compared to Team3’s 2 attacks. However, I am more inclined to believe that Team4’s attack was manual rather than automated.

Root cause

A good way to find the cause of the vulnerability is to trace it from backwards to head. This transaction resulted in a total loss of 596 WETH, which we can pinpoint as shown in the diagram: we found that the WETH was transferred from a victim 0x53...85 to the attacker’s contract, where the executeOperation() function was called.

Trace the internal transactions:

Caught it: Starting from executeOperation() as the attack entry point, it ultimately leads to deloopAllCollaterals(). This series of calls does not perform any checks on the arguments. It’s a classic arbitrary call vulnerability.

Even if we continue to chase up and find the entrance, there is no inspection:

This is the core of the attack:

  • The attacker can exploit the arbitrary call vulnerability to make the vulnerable contract execute arbitrary content. In this situation, many users have approved the vulnerable contract for this project. The hacker can then make the vulnerable contract transfer the approved funds from the users to themselves.
  • Having identified the problem, the next step is to find the victims—specifically, those who authorized the vulnerable contract and met the conditions for the attack. Identifying these victims is not simple, and it is also necessary to know how much they have approved and collateralized.
